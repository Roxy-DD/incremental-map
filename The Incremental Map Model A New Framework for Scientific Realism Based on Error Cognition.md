# The Incremental Map Model: A New Framework for Scientific Realism Based on Error Cognition

  

## Abstract

  

This paper proposes the Incremental Map Model, a new scientific methodology framework that addresses core challenges in the scientific realism debate and Kuhn's paradigm theory. By reinterpreting truth as a superposition state—drawing on quantum mechanical concepts—rather than an absolute entity, the model provides a novel defense of scientific realism against the pessimistic induction argument. The paper systematically analyzes multi-level sources of error in scientific cognition, including perceptual limitations, tool measurement errors, theoretical abstraction errors, and cognitive biases in induction. Building on this analysis, the model distinguishes between "point maps" (empirically verified experimental records with probability 1) and "line maps" (subjective theoretical conjectures). This distinction ensures the cumulative nature of scientific knowledge while preserving theoretical innovation, resolving the tension between stability and revolution in Kuhn's paradigm theory. The model also introduces a flexible archiving mechanism for theoretical conjectures, facilitating intergenerational knowledge transmission. By engaging with contemporary work in scientific realism, perspectival realism, and the philosophy of scientific practice, the Incremental Map Model offers both a theoretical framework and practical methodological tools for scientific evaluation and academic fairness.

  

**Keywords**: scientific methodology, scientific realism, error theory, paradigm theory, cognitive mapping, experimental records

  

---

  

## 1. Introduction

  

The nature and reliability of scientific knowledge remain central concerns in philosophy of science. The debate between scientific realism and anti-realism continues to generate sophisticated arguments (Chakravartty, 2017; Massimi, 2022), yet both sides struggle to reconcile scientific progress with the limitations of human cognition. Concurrently, Kuhn's paradigm theory (Kuhn, 1962) has challenged the cumulative view of scientific knowledge, highlighting how paradigm shifts can lead to the apparent collapse of established theoretical systems.

  

This paper addresses these interconnected challenges by proposing the Incremental Map Model, a new methodological framework that reinterprets the nature of truth and error in scientific cognition. The model's core innovation lies in distinguishing between two dimensions of scientific research: "point maps" (empirically verified experimental records) and "line maps" (subjective theoretical conjectures). This distinction provides a novel defense of scientific realism while resolving the tension between stability and revolution in Kuhn's paradigm theory.

  

Building on contemporary work in perspectival realism (Massimi, 2018) and the philosophy of scientific practice (Rouse, 2002), the Incremental Map Model offers both theoretical insights and practical tools. It operationalizes the scientific spirit of intellectual honesty by requiring clear separation between verified empirical foundations and speculative theoretical extensions, ensuring that scientific knowledge remains cumulative despite theoretical upheavals.

  

The paper proceeds as follows: Section 2 reinterprets truth as a superposition state, drawing on quantum mechanical concepts to address the pessimistic induction argument; Section 3 analyzes multi-level sources of error in scientific cognition; Section 4 presents the core framework of the Incremental Map Model; Section 5 engages with existing philosophy of science theories; Section 6 summarizes contributions and future research directions.

## 2. Truth as a Superposition State: A Critique of Traditional Truth Conceptions

  

### 2.1 The Dilemma of Traditional Truth Conceptions

  

In traditional discussions of philosophy of science, truth is usually understood as an absolute, unchanging entity. This view can be traced back to Aristotle's correspondence theory, which holds that truth is the correspondence between thought and reality. In modern philosophy of science, this view has been continued in the discourse of realists (Psillos, 1999). However, this traditional conception of truth faces serious theoretical difficulties.

  

First, the history of science shows that any scientific theory may be revised or replaced in subsequent development. From Newtonian mechanics to relativity, from classical physics to quantum mechanics, the evolution of scientific theories presents a characteristic of constantly approaching but never reaching ultimate truth (Laudan, 1984). If truth is an absolute, unchanging entity, then the history of scientific progress cannot be reasonably explained.

  

Second, the limitations of scientific cognition prevent us from directly accessing the so-called "ultimate truth." Human perceptual abilities, cognitive tools, and observation methods all have inherent limitations, which constitute an insurmountable gap between the cognitive subject and objective reality (Nagel, 1986). As Kant pointed out, we can only know phenomena, not things-in-themselves (Kant, 1781/1998).

  

### 2.2 The Argument for Truth as a Superposition State

  

Based on the above analysis, this paper argues that truth should be understood as a superposition state. Here, the concept of "superposition state" is borrowed from quantum mechanics, referring to a situation where a system exists in multiple possible states simultaneously (Bohr, 1928). Applying this concept to the problem of truth means that truth is not a single, deterministic entity, but a superposition state composed of countless possible truths.

  

This view can be supported from several aspects:

  

First, the hierarchical nature of scientific cognition determines the superposition state characteristic of truth. At different cognitive levels, truth approximations of different precisions are obtained. For example, Newtonian mechanics provides sufficiently precise descriptions at the macro scale; however, quantum mechanics provides more accurate pictures at the micro scale. These two theories are not simply right or wrong, but different approximations of truth at different levels (Cartwright, 1983).

  

Second, the dependence on observation conditions indicates the contextual nature of truth. The same physical phenomenon may exhibit different characteristics under different observation conditions. The observer effect in quantum mechanics shows that the act of observation itself affects the state of the observed system (Heisenberg, 1927). This means that truth obtained is always associated with specific observation conditions.

  

Third, the instrumental characteristics of scientific theories support the superposition state understanding of truth. As instrumentalism maintains, the main function of scientific theories is to predict and control empirical phenomena, rather than to describe objective reality (Duhem, 1906/1954). From this perspective, different theories can be seen as tools for dealing with different problems, and they have their value within their respective scopes of application.

  

### 2.3 The Philosophical Implications of the Superposition State Conception of Truth

  

Understanding truth as a superposition state has important philosophical implications. First, it provides a new defense path for scientific realism. One of the main challenges facing traditional realism is the "pessimistic induction" (Laudan, 1981), which argues that many successful scientific theories in history have later been proven false. If truth is a superposition state, then this pessimistic induction loses its critical force, because the replaced theory is not completely wrong, but is included in a more precise truth approximation. This view resonates with Massimi's (2022) "perspectival realism," which emphasizes the contextual and perspectival nature of scientific knowledge. In addition, this view is also consistent with Saatsi's (2018) theory of "realism and the limits of explanation," which argues that realism should focus on the limits of explanation rather than pursuing absolute truth.

  

Second, the superposition state conception of truth provides a reasonable explanation for scientific progress. Scientific progress is not a linear process from error to correctness, but a nonlinear process of continuously exploring more precise approximations in the superposition state of truth (Worrall, 1989). This explanation acknowledges both the cumulative nature of scientific knowledge and the possibility of scientific revolutions.

  

Finally, the superposition state conception of truth provides a new standard for theoretical evaluation in scientific practice. Instead of asking "Is this theory correct?" the question becomes "Under what conditions does this theory provide a truth approximation of what precision?" This evaluation method is more in line with the actual needs of scientific practice.

## 3. Multi-level Analysis of Error: From Tool Limitations to Cognitive Limitations

  

### 3.1 The Limitations of Traditional Error Conceptions

  

In traditional discussions of philosophy of science and scientific practice, error is usually understood as a problem of the precision of measurement tools. This view holds that by improving measurement tools and experimental design, errors can be gradually reduced and an accurate description of objective reality ultimately obtained (Hacking, 1983). However, this traditional error conception ignores deeper sources of error, especially inherent errors in the process of information transmission.

  

### 3.2 Inherent Errors in Information Transmission

  

This paper argues that error is not only related to the precision of measurement tools, but also directly related to the level of information transmission. Every time information is transmitted from one level to another, new errors are generated. This information transmission error can be divided into several levels:

  

The first level is perceptual error. When human senses directly receive external information, the processing process of the retina and brain itself generates errors. This error is an inherent property of the human perceptual system and cannot be completely eliminated (Gibson, 1979).

  

The second level is tool measurement error. When measurement tools are used, the measurement process of the tool generates the first error, and when the tool transmits the measurement result to human senses, it generates the second error. The superposition of these two levels of error makes the gap between the result of tool measurement and objective reality even larger (Franklin, 1986).

  

The third level is theoretical abstraction error. Scientific theories are abstractions and generalizations of empirical phenomena, and this abstraction process itself generates errors. Different abstraction methods produce different theoretical frameworks, and the differences between these theoretical frameworks often reflect errors in the abstraction process (Suppe, 1977).

  

The fourth level is transmission error. When scientific knowledge is transmitted through literature, education, and other channels, each transmission generates new errors. This transmission error makes the knowledge received different from the original knowledge (Bazerman, 1988).

  

### 3.3 Cognitive Limitations in Scientific Induction

  

In addition to the above inherent errors in information transmission, this paper argues that there is a more fundamental source of error in scientific cognition—cognitive limitations in scientific induction.

  

When a pattern is discovered through experimental observation, although experimental verification is conducted, this verification is usually not complete. The pattern is not verified in all possible cases, but a probability of 1 is subjectively assigned to these patterns based on limited experimental samples. This approach ignores the inherent uncertainty of inductive reasoning (Hume, 1748/2000) and is the largest source of error in scientific cognition.

  

This cognitive limitation manifests in various forms in scientific practice. For example, when experimental results do not conform to theoretical predictions, they are often attributed to experimental errors rather than questioning the correctness of the theory. This approach may lead to important scientific discoveries being overlooked (Holton, 1973). Another example is that when new experimental evidence emerges, attempts are often made to incorporate it into existing theoretical frameworks rather than considering constructing new theories. This conservative tendency may hinder scientific innovation (Kuhn, 1962).

  

### 3.4 The Cumulative Effect of Error

  

The above various sources of error are not independent of each other, but superpose and reinforce each other. Each level of information transmission increases error, and cognitive limitations amplify the impact of these errors. This cumulative effect makes the gap between scientific cognition and objective reality continue to expand.

  

However, this does not mean that scientific cognition is futile. Just as fish do not know fluid dynamics but know how to swim, although truth cannot be exhausted, it can be used (Ryle, 1949). The value of scientific practice does not lie in obtaining absolute truth, but in continuously reducing errors and improving cognitive precision of objective reality.

## 4. The Incremental Map Model: A Dual Architecture of Point Maps and Line Maps

  

### 4.1 The Limitations of Traditional Scientific Approaches

  

To more clearly understand the necessity of the Incremental Map Model, the limitations of traditional scientific approaches must first be analyzed. This paper analogizes the exploration process of scientific research to drawing a map: true truth is an objective, independent existence, and scientific research is about finding the most precise way to draw this map.

  

The traditional scientific approach is similar to "drawing lines." Researchers start from a point or a theorem and expand knowledge through continuous prediction and verification. However, this approach has serious problems:

  

First, when a paradigm breakthrough occurs, that is, when the starting point of this line is proven wrong, then the entire line needs to be redrawn. This leads to the non-cumulative characteristic of scientific knowledge (Kuhn, 1962).

  

Second, the middle of this line may be empty, not completely verified by experiments. Researchers often draw the entire line based on only a few experimental points, relying on conjecture and intuition. This leads to the inability to accurately identify reliable information even if it exists within it.

  

Third, the entire line is unstable and unreliable. The map drawn in this way is not only difficult to create, but also complex and chaotic. It is very likely that adjusting one line will cause the entire map to collapse.

  

### 4.2 The Core Framework of the Incremental Map Model

  

In response to the limitations of traditional scientific approaches, this paper proposes the Incremental Map Model. This model divides scientific research into two maps: point maps and line maps.

  

#### 4.2.1 Point Maps: The Foundation of Experimental Records

  

Point maps record the complete records of each experiment, including direct, non-abstract mathematical descriptions of experimental conditions, environments, operations, etc. The design of point maps follows the following principles:

  

First, the principle of "seeing is believing, experiments are true." Experiments are the closest way to truth, so point maps conduct inductive-free honest records of verifiable natural states. This principle embodies the core of the scientific spirit: only believe what you see and hear, without adding any subjective speculation.

  

Second, resolutely abandon induction. Instead of integrating and merging records of different scenarios and results, each experimental result is recorded as an independent point. This approach may seem cumbersome, but it is a persistence of the scientific spirit: scientists are unwilling to tell lies, unwilling to deceive themselves, so they do not exaggerate limited empirical verification into universal truth.

  

Third, records with probability 1. Each point in the point map records the verification of a pattern proven by experiments under specific conditions, so these records have a probability of 1. This strict probability definition is an honest attitude towards the laws of nature: scientists only confirm what they have seen with their own eyes, without exaggerating its scope of application.

  

Fourth, independently record different results. When different results occur under the same experimental conditions, they should be independently recorded as new points, rather than attributing them to experimental errors. These so-called "errors" may be the verification and innovation starting points of future new theories. Artificially attributing them to errors will lead to the loss of information. This approach embodies honesty towards the laws of nature: scientists acknowledge that they cannot fully understand all phenomena, so they honestly record all results, rather than selectively ignoring those that do not conform to their expectations.

  

Fifth, overlay update. When patterns are verified through more precise experimental methods, overlay tags can be added on top of the original points to update records, but the original experimental records are still retained. This is the path of experimental development and pattern verification. This approach not only embodies an open attitude towards scientific progress, but also retains honest respect for historical records.

  

Point maps are reliable databases that scientific research can trust. Any new experiment will only add points rather than causing the entire map to collapse. This design ensures the cumulative nature and stability of scientific knowledge. More importantly, it embodies the core of the scientific spirit: being honest with oneself, honest with the scientific enterprise, and honest with the laws of nature.

  

#### 4.2.2 Line Maps: Free Space for Theoretical Conjectures

  

Line maps are maps that everyone can freely modify and each scientist independently maintains. They are subjective line maps of patterns based on point maps, that is, human conjectures. The design of line maps follows the following principles:

  

First, the independence of scientists. Each scientist can plan experimental verification and update experimental plans based on their own line maps, that is, their predictions of patterns. This ensures the independence of scientists' thinking.

  

Second, the transfer of authority. What leads to authority is scientific results and experimental data themselves, not a certain scientist. This eliminates human factors and makes scientific research absolutely free and independent.

  

Third, the objectivity of point maps. Any scientist's experimental verification results can be updated to point maps, but point maps are not subjectively modified by anyone. This restores the identity of scientists: recording the laws of nature rather than defining them.

  

#### 4.2.3 The Archiving Mechanism of Line Maps: Intergenerational Transmission of Scientific Heritage

  

To ensure that unexperimentally verified but valuable theories in line maps are not forgotten over time, the Incremental Map Model introduces a flexible archiving mechanism. Scientists can freely choose whether to make their line maps public, and this design has multiple methodological values.

  

First, the scientific value of public archiving. When scientists choose to make their line maps public, these line maps will be officially recorded and permanently preserved, and any researcher can access them. This public archiving mechanism ensures that even if some theories cannot be experimentally verified under current technical conditions, their theoretical frameworks and verification paths will not be forgotten. These theories may contain important scientific insights, but are limited by the experimental conditions or technical means of the time.

  

Second, the continuity of knowledge in intergenerational transmission. Publicly archived line maps provide clear verification paths for future scientists. Even if the original scientists have been dead for many years, future researchers can still access the archived line maps to understand the predecessors' theoretical conceptions, prediction contents, and recommended experimental verification methods. This intergenerational transmission mechanism avoids the rupture of scientific knowledge and ensures the continuity of scientific exploration.

  

Third, theoretical activation after technological progress. The history of science shows that many theories could not be verified when proposed, but were later confirmed with technological progress. For example, when Einstein's general relativity was proposed in 1915, many predictions could not be verified, but with the development of observation technology, these predictions were later confirmed. The archiving mechanism of line maps provides institutional guarantee for the "activation" of such theories. When new experimental technologies emerge, researchers can immediately retrieve relevant archived line maps and conduct verification experiments.

  

Fourth, the balance between academic freedom and knowledge sharing. Scientists have the right to choose whether to make their line maps public, which respects their academic freedom. At the same time, incentive mechanisms for public archiving (such as higher academic recognition) encourage scientists to share their theoretical conceptions. This balance both protects scientists' intellectual property rights and promotes the sharing of scientific knowledge.

  

Fifth, standardized management of archived line maps. To ensure the retrievability and understandability of archived line maps, the Incremental Map Model requires that publicly archived line maps must include the following elements: clear statements of theoretical assumptions, specific descriptions of prediction contents, recommended experimental verification methods, associations with relevant experimental records in point maps, historical background of the theory, and literature citations. This standardized management ensures the academic quality and practical value of archived line maps.

  

Sixth, version tracking of archived line maps. Scientists can modify their line maps multiple times, and each modification will be recorded as a new version. This version tracking mechanism allows researchers to understand the development process of theories, compare differences between different versions, and thus better understand the evolution of scientists' thinking.

  

The archiving mechanism of line maps is an important part of the Incremental Map Model. It solves the problem of "theoretical forgetting" in traditional scientific research and provides institutional guarantee for the long-term accumulation and intergenerational transmission of scientific knowledge. This mechanism embodies the forward-looking nature and sustainability of the Incremental Map Model, ensuring the continuity and innovation of scientific exploration.

  

### 4.3 The Value of Combining Point Maps and Line Maps

  

The combination of point maps and line maps produces important methodological value:

  

First, point maps ensure the reliability and distinguishability of data, clearly separating scientific foundations from human conjectures. This ensures the rigor of science and ensures the transmission of information, because when explaining, we directly anchor to real experimental records rather than pure imagination.

  

Second, line maps ensure the independence of each scientist. No one needs to blindly follow a certain authoritative theory, nor do they need to be unable to publish or be suppressed because their theory contradicts a certain authority. This achieves true academic fairness.

  

Third, the redefinition of scientific contributions. Scientific contributions are judged by who has drawn more points, not who has drawn more beautiful lines. Even if your lines are elegant and have many verified points in the future, the value of lines is not as high as that of points. This ensures a direct shift in our previous concept: we used to think that the theory with wider coverage is more valuable, but now we distinguish between what you predict and what you have empirically verified.

  

Fourth, the clear separation of two value systems. We strongly encourage and support you to make predictions, conduct abstract mathematical or other theoretical research, but we only recognize the value you have experimentally verified, not the value of your predictions. These are two systems, just like we recognize that you have great potential in the future, but we also recognize that your current ability is only this much.

  

The combination of point maps and line maps makes value more authoritative, scientific, and standardized, clearly separating the two originally intertwined value systems and allowing them to exert their maximum value. This is a sustainable development path for science, rather than a chaotic mess that collapses entirely when a problem occurs.

## 5. Dialogue with Existing Philosophy of Science Theories

  

### 5.1 Contributions to Scientific Realism

  

The Incremental Map Model provides a new defense path for scientific realism. The main challenge facing traditional realism is the "pessimistic induction" (Laudan, 1981), which argues that many successful scientific theories in history have later been proven false. The Incremental Map Model responds to this challenge by distinguishing between point maps and line maps.

  

Each experimental record in point maps is a truth approximation with a probability of 1, and these records constitute the reliable foundation of scientific knowledge. Even if theories in line maps are replaced, experimental records in point maps remain valid. This means that scientific progress is not a linear process from error to correctness, but a process of continuously constructing more precise line maps based on point maps.

  

This understanding supports the view of structural realism (Worrall, 1989), which emphasizes the continuity of mathematical structures in the evolution of scientific theories. The Incremental Map Model further points out that the foundation of this continuity is the experimental records in point maps, rather than the theoretical expressions in line maps.

  

### 5.2 Complement to Kuhn's Paradigm Theory

  

Kuhn's paradigm theory (Kuhn, 1962) emphasizes the non-cumulative characteristic of scientific revolutions. The Incremental Map Model, while acknowledging scientific revolutions, provides a new understanding of the cumulative nature of scientific knowledge through the concept of point maps.

  

In the Incremental Map Model, scientific revolutions mainly occur at the level of line maps, while point maps remain stable. This means that although theoretical frameworks may undergo fundamental changes, experimental records remain valid. This understanding not only retains the accuracy of Kuhn's insights into scientific revolutions, but also avoids the relativistic tendencies that may be caused by his theory.

  

### 5.3 Response to the Philosophy of Scientific Practice

  

In recent years, the philosophy of scientific practice has emphasized that scientific knowledge is constructed in practice (Rouse, 2002; Soler et al., 2014). The Incremental Map Model is highly consistent with this view.

  

Point maps record specific operations and observation results in scientific practice, and these records are the foundation of scientific knowledge construction. Line maps reflect the theoretical conjectures formed by scientists in practice. By distinguishing between these two levels, the Incremental Map Model provides a new framework for understanding the complexity of knowledge construction in scientific practice.

  

### 5.4 Implications for Scientific Evaluation Methods

  

The Incremental Map Model has important implications for scientific evaluation methods. Traditional scientific evaluation is often based on the influence and coverage of theories, while the Incremental Map Model proposes that scientific contributions should mainly be based on the quantity and quality of experimental records in point maps.

  

This evaluation method is more in line with the actual needs of scientific practice and is more fair. It encourages scientists to conduct solid experimental work rather than merely pursuing theoretical novelty. At the same time, it provides institutional guarantee for academic fairness, because any scientist's experimental records can equally enter point maps, regardless of their theoretical status.

## 6. Conclusion

  

This paper proposes the Incremental Map Model as a new scientific methodology framework. This model provides new ideas for solving core problems in philosophy of science by distinguishing between point maps (experimental records) and line maps (theoretical conjectures).

  

The main contributions of this paper include:

  

First, reexamining the concept of truth and arguing that truth should be understood as a superposition state rather than an absolute, unchanging entity. This view provides a new defense path for scientific realism.

  

Second, systematically analyzing multi-level sources of error in scientific cognition, including inherent errors in information transmission and cognitive limitations in scientific induction. This analysis provides a new perspective for understanding the limitations of scientific cognition.

  

Third, proposing the Incremental Map Model, which divides scientific research into point maps and line maps. This model not only distinguishes between experimental foundations and theoretical conjectures, but also introduces a flexible archiving mechanism for line maps, providing operational methodological tools for theoretical evaluation, academic fairness, and knowledge transmission in scientific practice.

  

Fourth, engaging in dialogue with existing philosophy of science theories, including scientific realism, Kuhn's paradigm theory, and the philosophy of scientific practice. The Incremental Map Model responds to these theories while providing new supplements and optimizations, especially in the long-term accumulation and intergenerational transmission of scientific knowledge.

  

The Incremental Map Model has broad application prospects. In scientific practice, it can provide guidance for the standardization of experimental records and the fairness of theoretical evaluation. In scientific education, it can help students better understand the nature of scientific knowledge and the mechanism of scientific progress. In scientific policy, it can provide a theoretical foundation for the reform of scientific research evaluation systems.

  

Future research can be carried out in several directions. First, we can further explore the interaction mechanism between point maps and line maps, especially how experimental records affect theoretical conjectures and how theoretical conjectures guide experimental design. Second, we can study the applicability of the Incremental Map Model in different disciplinary fields and how to adjust the specific implementation of the model according to disciplinary characteristics. Finally, we can explore the integration of the Incremental Map Model with emerging scientific research paradigms (such as open science, data-driven science).

  

The Incremental Map Model is not only a theoretical framework, but also a practical guide. It reminds us that the value of science does not lie in obtaining absolute truth, but in continuously reducing errors and improving our cognitive precision of objective reality. In this sense, the Incremental Map Model embodies the core of the scientific spirit: humility, honesty, openness, and progress.
